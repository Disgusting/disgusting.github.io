1. Timing and Motion

Timing plays a critical role in creating immersive game experiences. One of the primary ways timing affects players is through its influence on flow states—mental conditions where players are fully absorbed in the game and lose track of time.

Non-linear timing

Non-linear easing, or “easing,” adds flair by controlling how motion speeds up or slows down. Techniques like overshooting, where movement extends slightly beyond its target, create visually satisfying animations. This exaggeration enhances simple actions, like opening a door, by adding weight and momentum, making interactions feel more impactful and realistic, boosting player satisfaction.

Anticipation and Dissipation

Anticipation is the building of tension before big moments, like a pause before a character swings a weapon.

Dissipation follows the action, slowing movement to give a sense of completion. Together, these timing elements reduce visual clutter and improve readability, creating a more immersive experience for players.

Motion attributes

Motion attributes refer to the specific characteristics of movement—such as speed, direction, rhythm, and smoothness—that can evoke emotions and enhance the aesthetic quality of animations. Insights from these attributes aim to elevate design in media, visualisations, interactive art, and immersive experiences, making them more emotionally engaging and visually appealing.
