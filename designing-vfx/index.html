Real-time Visual Effects Guidebook

In video games, visual effects (VFX) do more than add flair, they shape player experiences, guiding emotions, focus, and interaction. This guidebook delves into the core principles of real-time VFX design, exploring how motion, shape, and colour contribute to immersion and intrinsic rewards. By understanding these principles, VFX artists can craft effects that not only enhance gameplay but also deepen player engagement.




1. Motion

Timing plays a critical role in creating immersive game experiences. One of the primary ways timing affects players is through its influence on flow states—mental conditions where players are fully absorbed in the game and lose track of time.


Non-linear timing

Non-linear easing, or “easing,” adds flair by controlling how motion speeds up or slows down. Techniques like overshooting, where movement extends slightly beyond its target, create visually satisfying animations. This exaggeration enhances simple actions, like opening a door, by adding weight and momentum, making interactions feel more impactful and realistic, boosting player satisfaction.

Anticipation and Dissipation

Anticipation is the building of tension before big moments, like a pause before a character swings a weapon.

Dissipation follows the action, slowing movement to give a sense of completion. Together, these timing elements reduce visual clutter and improve readability, creating a more immersive experience for players.

Motion attributes

Motion attributes refer to the specific characteristics of movement—such as speed, direction, rhythm, and smoothness—that can evoke emotions and enhance the aesthetic quality of animations. Insights from these attributes aim to elevate design in media, visualisations, interactive art, and immersive experiences, making them more emotionally engaging and visually appealing.



2. Shape

Simplifying shapes and maintaining clear associations between objects helps players understand gameplay mechanics quickly and intuitively. This reduces confusion and enhances focus, making the gaming experience more streamlined and enjoyable.


Simple Shapes

Humans tend to perceive objects as whole forms before analysing their individual parts. This is closely related to the global-precedence hypothesis, which suggests that global shapes or overall structures are processed first, followed by the details or smaller components. In VFX design, this concept is applied to ensure that important visual cues are immediately understood by the player, reducing cognitive load and allowing them to focus on critical gameplay elements.

Distinguishable

The effect should be distinguishable from their background to ensuring that important visual elements stand out against their surroundings allows players to focus on key interactions and avoid being overwhelmed by unnecessary details.



3. Colour

Color plays a significant role in directing player attention and evoking emotional responses. Understanding the impact of colour saturation, palette choices, and the use of complementary and analogous colours helps VFX artists design visually cohesive effects that enhance the player's focus and experience.


Saturation Range

VFX that utilises a higher saturation range can draw more focus to important gameplay elements. However, it is important to avoid extreme saturation for regular in-game elements, reserving those extremes for UI or particularly significant events.

Hue Progression

The the concept of overshoot, where the action—be it physical or colour-based—exaggerates past the intended point before settling down. This technique can create more dynamic and engaging visual effects, drawing player attention through gradual colour transitions.

Colour Accent

Colour progression Overshoot can act as an "accent," giving additional flair and making these shifts more noticeable and satisfying.
